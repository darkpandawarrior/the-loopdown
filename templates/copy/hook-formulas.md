# Hook formulas

Line 1 does one job: stop the scroll. Steal these shapes; fill with a real detail.

| Formula | Shape | Example |
|---------|-------|---------|
| **The impossible number** | A concrete, absurd metric | "Our app clocked a parked user at 400 km/h." |
| **The confession** | Admit a mistake first | "I shipped a race condition to 50k users. Twice." |
| **The contrarian correction** | "X is the wrong default" | "expect/actual is the wrong default in KMP." |
| **The hidden cost** | "X is quietly doing Y" | "collectAsState is quietly leaking your coroutines." |
| **The one-line reason** | Promise a single cause | "Your LazyColumn recomposes on every scroll. Here's the one line." |
| **The reframe** | Rename a familiar thing | "GPS isn't a source of truth. It's a witness with a concussion." |
| **The stakes** | What breaks if you're wrong | "A payments SDK has no room for 'probably'." |
| **The before/after** | Two numbers, a gap | "50% → 95% accuracy. Not with a better sensor." |
| **The question** | Ask the reader's pain | "Ever watched your ANR rate spike and had no idea why?" |

## Rules
- Specific > clever. A real number beats a vague boast.
- One idea in the hook. Save the nuance for the body.
- If line 1 needs line 2 to make sense, line 1 isn't the hook yet.
- The wordplay/reframe hooks are the most *you* — lean on the reframe. (voice-profile #2, #5)

## CTA endings (pick one, rotate)
- "What's a signal your system learned to distrust? 👇"
- "What would you have done differently?"
- "Tell me I'm wrong. (Genuinely — I'll learn something.)"
- Or no CTA — a clean landed payload often outperforms a forced question.
